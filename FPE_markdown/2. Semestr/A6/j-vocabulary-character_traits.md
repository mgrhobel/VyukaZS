**ADJECTIVES DESCRIBING CHARACTER**

\

When these **attributes,** or **qualities**, are long-term and seem to
be part of the person, they are known as character "**traits**."
Sometimes they are called good or bad "qualities." Almost all of these
adjectives have nouns which actually name the trait, but people more
often use the adjective to describe a person.

\

Note: "character" is a slightly different concept from "personality,"
but this is not always so easy to distinguish. People also say
"personality traits," but more often for things like friendliness,
aloofness, optimism, pessimism, etc. Some character traits are clearly
positive, and usually have a negative opposite (e.g. reliable,
unreliable). But some traits can be seen as either good or bad depending
on your point of view. E.g. what is "strong-willed" or "determined" for
one person, might be seen as "obstinate/stubborn" by another.

\

Words that seem most commonly used for these traits are in **bold.**
These are the most important to learn.

Words with \* are given in the textbook (*face2face*, A6)

\

**Notes:** A few of these words are misleadingly similar to Czech words.
Be careful!

\

**Competent** is not used the same way in English and Czech. In English,
"competent" means to have the ability, skills, knowledge, etc. to do a
job properly and well. E.g., She is a very competent manager. (English
uses "competent" in the Czech way only in very limited situations
characterized by the use of formal legal English.)

\

**Temperamental** in English is never positive. It is used to describe
someone who has a tendency to "fly" into a rage (get very angry very
quickly), or to react in an overly melodramatic way to feeling they have
been insulted or otherwise treated badly. It is mainly used to describe
the character of opera singers, actors, etc, who cause problems by being
temperamental. On the other hand, everyone has a temperament. This is
not good or bad, but must be further described by an adjective. E.g. My
father has a very calm temperament. (A person whose mood goes up and
down unpredictably would be described as having a volatile or changeable
temperament.)

\

Keep in mind that the words grouped together do not necessarily mean
exactly the same thing, nor can they always be used in the same
situations. Some of the words are synonymous, and can be used
interchangeably, but many have slightly different connotations. This
means they cannot all be used in the same context. Use a monolingual
dictionary for help with these distinctions. More practically, pay
attention to how these words are used when you read or listen to spoken
English.
